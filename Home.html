Cryptology Historically

Cryptology is a discipline with a history dating back to ancient times, encompassing a range of uses rooted in the need for secret communication and encryption. The encryption methods employed by the Egyptians around 1900 BCE represent some of the earliest applications in history and have diversified over time. During the Middle Ages, ciphers, particularly used in diplomatic communication, facilitated the transmission of confidential information among royal families and states. One of the most famous instances of codebreaking occurred during World War II when the German Enigma machine was deciphered, underscoring the significance of cryptography throughout history. In the modern era, with the advancement of computers and the widespread adoption of digital communication, cryptography has become more intricate, forming the foundation for secure communication systems.

Cipher Types

Ciphers represent encryption algorithms used for securely transmitting confidential information. In this field, various types of ciphers exist, including:

  • Symmetric Encryption: The same key is used for both encryption and decryption processes. For example, DES (Data Encryption Standard) and AES (Advanced Encryption Standard) are examples of symmetric encryption.
  • Public Key Encryption: Every user has a public key and a private key. Public keys are used for encrypting information, while private keys are used for decryption. RSA (Rivest–Shamir–Adleman) is an example of this type of encryption.
  • Asymmetric Encryption: İki farklı anahtarın kullanıldığı bir şifreleme yöntemidir. Bu anahtarlar birbirine matematiksel olarak bağlıdır, ancak bir tanesi diğerini tespit etmek için kullanılamaz. Bu, güvenli bir iletişim sağlamak için kullanılır. Asimetrik şifreleme örnekleri arasında RSA ve ECC (Elliptic Curve Cryptography) bulunur.
  • Block Encryption and Flow Encryption: Block cipher encrypts data by dividing it into specific-sized blocks, encrypting each block separately. Stream cipher, on the other hand, encrypts data bit by bit. Both types ensure secure communication using different algorithms and methods.

Cipher types continue to evolve continuously for the purpose of secure communication and data protection. These encryption methods undergo constant evolution in conjunction with technological advancements in the field of information security.

Caesar Cipher

The Caesar Cipher is a simple encryption technique that encrypts each letter by shifting it a certain number of positions. Both encryption and decryption processes involve shifting each letter by a specific "key" number. For example, applying a 3-letter shift (key = 3) to the text: "HELLO" would be encrypted as "KHOOR". Decrypting "KHOOR" with the same key would revert it back to "HELLO". Despite being a basic encryption method, the Caesar Cipher is one of the historically used techniques and represents one of the earliest encryption methods.

RSA-cipher

RSA is a public-key signature algorithm developed by Ron Rivest, Adi Shamir, and Leonard Adleman. The algorithm's initial paper was published in 1977, and it employs logarithmic functions to both complicate the process and provide resistance against brute-force attacks. AES-Cipher

Established by the U.S. National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) in 2001, Advanced Encryption Standard (AES) is a widely used encryption specification known for its superior strength compared to DES and triple DES, despite being more complex to implement. AES functions as a block cipher with key sizes available in 128, 192, and 256 bits, encrypting data in 128-bit blocks. The encryption process takes a 128-bit input, and through a substitution-permutation network principle, it produces a 128-bit encrypted cipher text output. Notably, AES operates on bytes of data, processing 128 bits (or 16 bytes) of input data at a time due to its 128-bit block size.
